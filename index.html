Alessandra Pandolfi (2000, IT) is a multidisciplinary designer whose approach intertwines field research, visual languages, and participatory or performative methodologies. She studied Design at the University of the Republic of San Marino and earned a Master’s in Social Design from the Design Academy Eindhoven.

Her current research focuses on the role of design within contemporary governance dynamics, examining its impact on rights, collective behaviors, and public spaces. Through situated practices and tools for collective reflection, she works on activating alternative imaginaries that foster dialogue and make complex topics — often perceived as abstract or distant — more tangible and accessible.

Ministry of Procedural Emergencies MA Graduation project

Social Design — Design Academy Eindhoven
June 2025

The Ministry of Procedural Emergencies stages a fictional governmental body that examines how emergency laws – once exceptional – are becoming normalized instruments of governance. Using Italy as a case study, the project explores how decree-laws passed since 2022 increasingly regulate everyday life, from migration and protest to urban security. Through an interactive installation, visitors step into the role of Exceptional Supervisors, engaging in bureaucratic rituals such as form-filling, reviewing emergency protocols, and analysing real legal decrees. These administrative gestures invite civic reflection on who defines urgency, how authority operates, and what forms of resistance or reimagination might exist. By mirroring the language of institutional urgency, the Ministry reveals the distance between citizens and the systems that govern them, reframing bureaucracy as a space for collective critique and imagination.

Project research, concept and design by Alessandra Pandolfi

When does the Exception become the Rule? MA Thesis

Social Design — Design Academy Eindhoven
printed April 2025

Uncovering the exceptional normality of bureaucratic systems, counteracting it through socio-political approaches and creative methodologies.

In a world increasingly governed by emergencies, this thesis questions how temporary crisis measures have quietly become permanent fixtures within democratic systems. Focusing on Italy as a case study, it explores how the frequent use of decree-laws bypasses traditional democratic processes, centralizing power under the guise of urgency.  Through political theory, historical research, and creative intervention strategies, the project uncovers how bureaucratic mechanisms perpetuate inequality and institutional mistrust. Ultimately, it advocates for grassroots activism, participatory governance, and critical design methodologies as tools to counteract the normalization of authoritarian practices and reclaim democratic agency.

Thesis research, writing and design by Alessandra Pandolfi
With the supervision of Vera Sacchetti
